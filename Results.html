Introduction:

 

The University hospital collects databases from patients who visit their outpatient clinic for collagen diseases, which are auto-immune diseases where patients generate antibodies that attack their own bodies. Thrombosis is a severe and potentially fatal complication of collagen diseases and is closely related to anti-cardiolipin antibodies. It is a medical emergency, and it is crucial to be able to detect and predict the possibility of it occurring.  Thrombosis is a medical condition in which blood clots form within the blood vessels, leading to a range of potentially serious health problems. The datasets for discovering regularities in thrombosis have been donated for analysis.

 

The Datasets:

 

The three tables provided in the dataset have information on patients, their laboratory test results, and their medical history. The database consists of three tables: tsumoto_a.csv, tsumoto_b.csv, and tsumoto_c.csv. The patients in these tables are connected by their ID number.

 

The tsumoto_a.csv table provides basic information about all patients, which includes their demographic details, medical history, and other relevant information. This table forms the starting point for exploring the patient data.

 

The tsumoto_b.csv table contains information on special laboratory examinations that were performed on a subset of patients. The results of these tests can help in the diagnosis of certain conditions, including collagen diseases, and provide valuable insights into the health of the patients. This table includes various measures of coagulation, such as KCT, RVVT, and LAC, as well as ANA Pattern and aCL IgA anti-Cardiolipin antibody (IgA) test results. It also includes a multivalued attribute for diagnosis and symptoms, including the degree of thrombosis.

 

The tsumoto_c.csv table provides information on laboratory examinations stored in the hospital information systems, including the results of routine laboratory tests and their dates. This table includes various measures of blood components, such as hemoglobin, platelet, and white blood cell counts, as well as liver and kidney function tests and measures of coagulation, such as PT, APTT, and FG. It also includes information on the presence of certain antibodies, including anti-DNA, anti-ribonuclear protein, and anti-SSA.

 

After cleaning the datasets, we merge the data from different tables to create a comprehensive patient profile. We consider only the patients that are common in all three datasets. Additionally, we create new columns in the dataset, which are as follows:

 

 

1.   Age: This is the age of the patient in the database on the date of the thrombosis test.

2.   Thrombosis_bol: It is a Boolean variable indicating if a patient has thrombosis or not.

3.   Thrombosistestyears: It calculates the time distance between the standard laboratory examination and the thrombosis test.

3.   What is the relationship between the levels of Lactate Dehydrogenase (LDH) and the Years from the Thrombosis Test?

 

Figure 3 - Lactate Dehydrogenase vs Thrombosis Test Years

 

The study aimed to identify any characteristic time patterns related to thrombosis by defining a derived attribute called Thrombosis Test Years, which is the time distance between the standard laboratory examination and the thrombosis test. The findings showed that the average Lactate Dehydrogenase values increased leading up to the date of thrombosis diagnosis and then decreased again. This pattern can be observed if one has a keen eye and uses a bit of imagination.

 

Specifically, the study found that as the time distance between the laboratory examination and the thrombosis test decreased, the average Lactate Dehydrogenase values gradually increased. This increase continued until the date of thrombosis diagnosis, after which the average Lactate Dehydrogenase values started to decrease again. This trend suggests that Lactate Dehydrogenase values can potentially be used as a marker to predict the occurrence of thrombosis, as it appears to be elevated leading up to the thrombosis event.

 

Overall, this finding suggests that regular monitoring of Lactate Dehydrogenase levels may be beneficial for those who are at high risk of developing thrombosis.

Overall, these results suggest that monitoring uric acid and urea nitrogen levels may be useful in identifying individuals who are at higher risk for developing thrombosis.
